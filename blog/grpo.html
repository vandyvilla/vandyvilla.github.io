A Practical GRPO Reference: Metrics to Watch and Configs to Tune

A field reference for training language models with GRPO — every metric worth logging (with healthy/abnormal shapes and fixes) and every configuration knob (with ranges and when to turn it).

Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) replaces PPO's learned value critic with a group-relative advantage: sample several completions per prompt, and score each one against its group's mean. That makes it cheaper and simpler — but it also introduces failure modes you only catch if you're watching the right signals. This post is the reference I keep open during runs: the metrics worth logging, and the configuration knobs worth tuning, each tied to the symptom that tells you to act.

The two halves are cross-referenced: when a metric goes abnormal, the "Mitigation" column names a knob; the configuration table's "When to adjust" column names the symptom. Read them together.

This reference is the operational companion to two other posts: for the underlying theory and formulas, see RL & GRPO Fundamentals; to see every metric and knob in working code, start with the hands-on GRPO from Scratch: A Code Walkthrough, which builds the whole training loop end to end and produces the very curves this reference describes.

Part 1 — Metrics to watch during training¶

A reference for what to log during GRPO training, what each signal means, the shape it should take when training is healthy, and what to do when it isn't. Organized by category. "Old policy" = the policy that generated the current rollout batch; "reference" = the frozen anchor (usually the SFT checkpoint) used for the KL penalty.

1. Reward & task-performance metrics¶

Metric What it measures Expected (healthy) shape Abnormal shape Mitigation
Mean reward (batch / group) Average programmatic reward over all sampled completions. The headline objective. Rises from baseline, then plateaus near the reward ceiling. Noisy but trending up. (a) Flat at baseline. (b) Rises then collapses. (c) Climbs while task quality stagnates. (a) Reward too sparse / verifier broken → add shaping, check verifier on hand-written samples. (b) Instability → lower lr, raise kl_beta, fewer inner epochs. (c) Reward hacking → see eval-gap row.
Task accuracy / pass rate (held-out) Independent correctness metric on a fixed eval set, not the training reward. Tracks mean reward upward; plateaus at task ceiling. Diverges below reward (reward up, accuracy flat/down). Reward hacking — the reward has an exploit. Patch the verifier, read high-scoring rollouts, add anti-exploit penalties.
pass@k (k>1) Fraction of prompts solved by at least one of k samples. Measures retained diversity/coverage. Rises; stays meaningfully above pass@1. Collapses toward pass@1. Diversity collapse / over-exploitation. Raise entropy bonus, increase sampling temperature, lower kl_beta cautiously, reduce inner epochs.
Reward-component breakdown Each additive reward term logged separately (e.g. format vs. correctness vs. length penalty). Each component improves in a sensible order (e.g. format saturates first, then correctness). One easy component saturates while the hard one never moves. Sparse-reward local optimum (the easy term is a "safe harbor"). Densify the hard term (graded credit), curriculum, or SFT warm-start.
Reward std (within batch) Spread of rewards across all completions in the step. Moderate early (lots to learn), shrinks as policy converges. Near zero from the start. No learning signal anywhere: all completions score alike. Raise temperature / group size, fix/shape reward, warm-start.
Train–eval reward gap Difference between training reward and held-out eval reward. Small and stable. Widens over training. Overfitting to the reward model / verifier quirks. Regularize (KL), refresh/ensemble the reward model, add held-out early stopping.

2. GRPO-specific group signals¶

These are the metrics unique to GRPO's critic-free, group-relative design. Watch them closely — they explain why the gradient is or isn't flowing.

Metric What it measures Expected (healthy) shape Abnormal shape Mitigation
Advantage mean Mean of group-normalized advantages. Should be ≈0 by construction (centered per group). Flat at ≈0 throughout. Persistently nonzero / drifting. Bug in centering (wrong group axis, leaked padding tokens into the mean). Audit the advantage computation.
Advantage std Spread of advantages across the batch — the effective gradient magnitude. Rises while there's much to learn, decays toward 0 at convergence. ≈0 from step one. Groups have no internal reward contrast. Raise temperature / group size, densify reward.
Fraction of zero-variance groups Share of prompts where all G completions earn the same reward (advantage ≡ 0, no gradient). GRPO's most underrated metric. Low early; rises toward 1.0 as accuracy reaches ceiling (= genuine convergence). Rises toward 1.0 while accuracy is mediocre. Learning has silently died: policy confidently commits to one (wrong) answer per prompt. Raise entropy bonus + temperature, increase G, densify reward, sync/relax reference.
Group size effective coverage Mean number of distinct completions per group (diversity within a group). Stays well above 1; only shrinks near convergence. Collapses to ~1 early. Premature determinism. Higher temperature, entropy bonus, larger G.
Reward range per group (max−min) Whether each group contains both good and bad samples to contrast. Healthy spread for most groups. Most groups flat. Same as zero-variance groups — the contrast that drives GRPO is gone.

3. Policy-divergence metrics¶

Metric What it measures Expected (healthy) shape Abnormal shape Mitigation
KL(policy ‖ reference) How far the policy has moved from the frozen anchor. The leash. Use the unbiased non-negative k3 estimator: exp(r−l) − (r−l) − 1. Spikes during fast learning, then settles to a small steady value. (a) Monotonic unbounded growth. (b) Pinned at ≈0 while reward is flat. (a) Policy running away → raise kl_beta, lower lr, fewer inner epochs. (b) Leash too tight, learning frozen → lower kl_beta, or sync the reference forward (from-scratch setting).
Approx KL(policy ‖ old) Per-update off-policyness between current and rollout policy (PPO's internal KL). Small, bounded within a step's inner epochs. Grows across inner epochs / between steps. Rollouts too stale. Reduce inner_epochs, shrink clip_eps, regenerate rollouts more often (smaller batch reuse).
Negative-KL fraction Share of tokens where a naive r−l KL estimate goes negative (variance diagnostic). ≈0 with the k3 estimator. Frequent with a naive estimator. Switch to k3 estimator; high values also flag very off-policy updates.

4. Exploration metrics¶

Metric What it measures Expected (healthy) shape Abnormal shape Mitigation
Policy entropy Average next-token entropy of the policy distribution — exploration pressure. Decays gradually as the policy sharpens around good outputs. (a) Cliff to ≈0 in early training. (b) Stuck high / rising. (a) Premature entropy collapse → entropy bonus (annealed high→low), higher temperature, lr decay. (b) No convergence / reward too flat → check reward signal; reduce entropy bonus if over-applied.
Per-position entropy Entropy broken down by token position in the completion. Sensible profile (e.g. higher at decision points, low at forced tokens). Uniformly collapsed, or pathologically high at answer tokens. Localizes where exploration died; tune temperature/bonus, inspect those positions' rollouts.

5. PPO surrogate mechanics¶

Metric What it measures Expected (healthy) shape Abnormal shape Mitigation
Importance ratio ρ = π/π_old (mean / max) How far each update has moved per-token probabilities from the rollout policy. Mean ≈1; max bounded (within a small multiple). Mean drifts from 1; max explodes (e.g. >10). Updates too aggressive / rollouts stale. Lower lr, fewer inner epochs, tighter clip_eps. Huge max-ratio often means a numerical/log-prob masking bug.
Clip fraction Fraction of tokens whose ratio hit the 1±clip_eps boundary (gradient clipped). Low single-digit %, stable. With inner_epochs=1 it's ≈0 and uninformative. Sustained spikes / large fraction. Updates exceeding the trust region. Lower lr, fewer inner epochs, or accept a smaller clip_eps. Isolated spike on resume = fresh optimizer moments.
Surrogate loss The clipped policy-gradient objective term itself. Trends consistent with reward improvement; noisy. Diverges to large magnitude / NaN. Numerical instability. Grad clipping, lower lr, check for inf/NaN in log-probs (masking, empty completions).
Fraction of tokens with zero gradient Share of completion tokens contributing no gradient (clipped on the wrong side, or zero advantage). Modest. Large. Combined effect of zero-variance groups + clipping; revisit reward contrast and trust-region width.

6. Optimization metrics¶

Metric What it measures Expected (healthy) shape Abnormal shape Mitigation
Total loss Full GRPO objective: −surrogate + β·KL − α·entropy. Noisy; not necessarily monotone (it's an RL objective, not supervised). Marches deterministically to the worst possible value; or NaN. A determined move toward worst-case reward usually means a sign error in the loss/advantage/gradient. Verify loss drops on a frozen batch; check a known-good sample's log-prob rises after one update. NaN → grad clip + lower lr.
Gradient norm (pre-clip) Magnitude of the raw gradient before clipping. Bounded; roughly tracks the lr schedule; spikes early then settles. (a) Repeated spikes. (b) ≈0. (a) Instability → grad clipping (max_grad_norm), lower lr. (b) No signal reaching params → check advantage std, masking, and that grads aren't all clipped away.
Gradient-clip frequency How often the global grad norm exceeds the clip threshold. Occasional early, rare later. Almost every step. Threshold too low or genuine instability. Raise threshold modestly; if instability, lower lr / fewer inner epochs.
Learning rate The scheduled lr (log it even though it's not learned). Follows your warmup/decay schedule. Flat when you intended decay; discontinuities. Verify scheduler is stepping; discontinuities on resume = scheduler/optimizer state not restored.
Parameter / update norm ratio ‖update‖ / ‖params‖ per step. Small and stable (e.g. ~1e-3). Large or growing. Effective step size too big. Lower lr; check optimizer state restored correctly after checkpoints.

7. Generation & output-quality metrics¶

Metric What it measures Expected (healthy) shape Abnormal shape Mitigation
Mean response length Average completion length in tokens. Stable / task-appropriate. Drifting upward over training. Classic length-bias reward hacking (longer = higher reward by artifact). Length-normalize the reward/advantage, add a length penalty, audit the reward model.
Truncation rate Fraction of completions hitting max_new_tokens without EOS. Low and stable. Rising. Policy learning to run on (often tied to length hacking). Raise max length to confirm it's not a cap artifact, then penalize non-termination / length.
EOS / completion rate Fraction of well-formed, properly terminated outputs. Rises early, saturates near 1. Stuck low. Format not being learned. Add a format reward component, SFT warm-start, inspect rollouts.
Repetition / n-gram diversity Degenerate repetition within or across completions (e.g. distinct-n, repeat rate). Low repetition, healthy diversity. Rising repetition, diversity collapse. Reward exploit via repetition, or entropy collapse. Add repetition penalty, raise entropy bonus/temperature, check reward model isn't rewarding boilerplate.
Output-distribution drift Qualitative read of actual sampled completions over time (not a scalar). Coherent, on-task, varied. Subtly gaming the reward, mode-collapsed, off-task. Always read raw rollouts — curves hide exploits and local optima that a glance at outputs reveals instantly. Adjust reward/verifier accordingly.

Cross-cutting debugging heuristics¶

  • Read the rollouts, not just the curves. Many failure modes (reward hacking, format local optima, repetition) are invisible in scalar metrics but obvious in 10 sampled outputs.
  • Reward → its minimum, deterministically = sign error in loss/advantage/gradient.
  • Reward up, eval flat/down = reward hacking; trust the independent eval, fix the reward.
  • Plateau + entropy≈0 + zero-variance groups rising + mediocre accuracy = premature collapse; inject exploration (entropy bonus, temperature, larger G) and/or densify reward.
  • KL exploding / clip fraction spiking / ratio max blowing up = updates too off-policy; lower lr, fewer inner epochs, tighter clip, stronger/relaxed KL as appropriate.
  • Supervised capacity probe: if RL stalls, train the same network supervised on the same task briefly. ~100% supervised ⇒ the bottleneck is exploration/reward, not model capacity.

Part 2 — Configuration parameters to tune¶

Every knob that matters for GRPO, organized by subsystem: what it does, a normal value range, and the signal that tells you it's time to change it. Ranges assume the common setting of fine-tuning a pretrained LLM unless noted; from-scratch toy training (like the notebook demo) often uses larger LRs and entropy bonuses. Watch the metrics in the companion metrics sheet to decide when to turn each of these.

1. Rollout & sampling¶

Parameter Effect Normal range When to adjust
group_size (G, num_generations) Completions sampled per prompt; the population the group-relative advantage is computed over. Larger G = lower-variance advantage estimates and more exploration, at linear compute cost. 4–64 (commonly 8–16; DeepSeekMath used 64) Raise when zero-variance-group fraction is high or advantage std is low (groups lack contrast). Lower to cut rollout cost if groups already show healthy spread.
prompts_per_step Distinct prompts per optimization step. With G, sets effective batch = prompts × G. Controls gradient-estimate stability across tasks. 8–256+ (hardware-bound) Raise if step-to-step reward is very noisy. Lower if memory-bound or to iterate faster.
max_new_tokens (max completion length) Hard cap on generated tokens per completion. Bounds compute and truncation behavior. Task-dependent: 8–64 short tasks; 1k–8k+ reasoning Raise if truncation rate is high and answers are genuinely getting cut off. Keep tight for short-answer tasks to save compute.
max_prompt_length Cap on prompt tokens (left-truncation if exceeded). Fits your dataset (512–4k typical) Raise if prompts are being silently truncated; lower to save memory.
temperature Sampling temperature during rollout. Primary exploration knob — higher = more diverse groups. 0.7–1.2 (often 1.0) Raise when entropy/diversity collapses early or groups go uniform. Lower if rollouts are incoherent/off-task.
top_p / top_k / min_p Nucleus / top-k / min-p truncation of the sampling distribution. Trades diversity for quality. top_p 0.9–1.0; top_k off or 50; min_p 0–0.05 Loosen (higher top_p, lower top_k) if diversity is too low; tighten if rollouts are degenerate. Keep generous so groups retain variation.
rollout/eval temperature split Separate (low) temperature for evaluation vs. (higher) for training rollouts. eval ≈ 0–0.3; train ≈ 0.7–1.0 Always log both; use greedy/low-temp eval so reported accuracy isn't inflated or hidden by sampling noise.

2. Advantage computation (GRPO core)¶

Parameter Effect Normal range When to adjust
norm_std (std normalization on/off) If on: A=(r−μ)/(σ+ε) (original GRPO). If off: A=r−μ (Dr. GRPO) — avoids amplifying reward noise when a group's σ is tiny. Off (Dr. GRPO) recommended; on only if rewards are well-scaled per group Turn off if training thrashes / sampled accuracy oscillates despite signal. Keep on only when group reward scales vary a lot and you want auto-scaling.
adv_eps Stabilizer ε in the std denominator (only used when norm_std=on). 1e-8 – 1e-4 Raise if you see exploding advantages from near-zero σ; irrelevant when std-norm is off.
advantage whitening (batch-level) Optionally standardize advantages across the whole batch after group centering. Off for GRPO (group centering already done) Generally leave off in GRPO; double-normalizing can wash out cross-group signal.
loss aggregation (token-mean vs. sequence-mean) Whether per-token losses are averaged within a sequence then over sequences (length-balanced) or pooled token-level. Affects length bias. Token-level (Dr. GRPO) reduces length bias; per-sequence-mean is the original Switch to token-level / length-normalized if mean response length drifts upward (length hacking).
reward scaling / clipping Rescale or clip raw rewards before advantage. Keeps gradients well-conditioned. Clip to a sane band; scale to O(1) Apply if reward magnitudes are wild or one component dominates; otherwise leave raw.

3. PPO surrogate & trust region¶

Parameter Effect Normal range When to adjust
clip_eps (ε) Trust-region width on the importance ratio 1±ε. Limits how far one update moves per token. 0.1–0.3 (0.2 standard) Lower if ratio max / clip fraction spike or KL explodes. Raise slightly if updates are too timid and learning crawls.
clip_eps_high / clip_eps_low (decoupled clipping) Asymmetric clip bounds. "Clip-higher" (DAPO) raises the upper bound to keep promoting low-probability good tokens and preserve entropy. low ≈ 0.2; high ≈ 0.22–0.28 Decouple and raise the high bound if entropy collapses early and you're losing exploration.
inner_epochs (μ, ppo_epochs) Gradient passes over each rollout batch. More = better sample efficiency but more off-policy drift. 1–4 (often 1) Reduce to 1 if approx-KL(policy‖old) or clip fraction grows across epochs. Increase only if rollouts are expensive and the policy stays near-on-policy.
mini-batch size (inner update) Sub-batch size for each gradient step within the rollout batch. Divides the rollout batch; hardware-bound Smaller for memory; larger for more stable per-step gradients. Keep ≥ a few groups so advantages are representative.
loss type (clip vs. token-level variants) Some recipes use token-level policy loss (DAPO) instead of per-sequence to fix length/format biases. Standard clipped surrogate; token-level for long-CoT Switch when long-completion tasks show length or formatting pathologies.

4. KL penalty & reference policy¶

Parameter Effect Normal range When to adjust
kl_beta (β, KL coefficient) Strength of the leash to the reference policy. Prevents drift/forgetting; too high freezes learning. 0.0–0.1 (many reasoning recipes use 0.001–0.04; some set 0) Raise if KL grows unbounded or the model drifts off-distribution / loses general ability. Lower (or zero) if the leash is choking learning and you have other stabilizers.
kl_estimator (k1 / k2 / k3) Formula for the per-token KL. k3 = exp(r−l)−(r−l)−1 is unbiased and always ≥ 0 (low variance). k3 Use k3 by default; switch only for debugging. High negative-KL fraction with k1 signals you should be on k3.
KL placement (in-reward vs. in-loss) Whether KL is subtracted from the reward (per-token shaping) or added as a separate loss term. In-loss is common in modern GRPO; in-reward is the classic RLHF style Move KL into the loss if reward-shaping placement complicates advantage interpretation; keep consistent with your advantage definition.
target_kl (adaptive β) If set, β is auto-tuned to hold KL near a target (early-stop or controller). target KL 0.01–0.1 if used Enable when a fixed β is hard to balance — the controller raises β when KL overshoots, relaxes it when under.
ref_sync_every (reference refresh) How often the frozen reference is updated to the current policy. Never = anchor to SFT/init; periodic = moving anchor. Never (RLHF on pretrained); every N steps for from-scratch Sync periodically if anchoring to a random/weak init fights learning. Keep fixed when the reference is a good SFT checkpoint you don't want to drift from.
use_reference / KL on-off Whether to include a KL term at all. Some GRPO variants drop it entirely and rely on clipping + entropy. On for RLHF; sometimes off for verifiable-reward RL Drop KL only when the reward is fully verifiable and clipping/entropy already stabilize training — and watch for drift if you do.

5. Exploration (entropy)¶

Parameter Effect Normal range When to adjust
ent_coef (α, entropy bonus) Reward for higher policy entropy — direct pressure against premature determinism. 0.0–0.01 (LLM RLHF); higher (0.01–0.1) for from-scratch / small models Raise if entropy cliffs to ≈0 early and zero-variance groups climb with poor accuracy. Lower if the policy won't converge / stays too random.
entropy annealing schedule Decays α over training (high exploration early → exploitation late). Linear/cosine from start value → ~0.003–0 Use whenever a static bonus either over-explores late or under-explores early. Anneal to near-zero by the last ~20% of steps.
min/target entropy (controller) Optionally adapt α to hold entropy above a floor. Floor set per task Enable if manual α tuning is brittle; the controller raises α when entropy dips below the floor.

6. Optimization & schedule¶

Parameter Effect Normal range When to adjust
learning_rate Step size. The single most impactful stability knob. LLM fine-tune: 1e-7 – 1e-5 (often 1e-6). From-scratch small: 1e-4 – 3e-3 Lower if grad norm / KL / clip fraction spike or loss diverges/NaNs. Raise cautiously if learning is glacial and everything else is stable.
lr schedule Constant, warmup→constant, or warmup→cosine/linear decay. Warmup then constant or cosine decay Add warmup if early steps are unstable; add decay if late training oscillates around the optimum.
warmup_steps / ratio Ramps LR from ~0 to avoid early instability while optimizer state is cold. 0–10% of total steps Increase if the first few hundred steps show grad/KL spikes.
total steps / epochs Training duration. RL plateaus and can degrade if run too long. Until eval plateaus Stop when held-out eval plateaus or train–eval gap widens; extend only if still improving.
max_grad_norm Global gradient-norm clip threshold. Core instability guard. 0.5–1.0 Lower if grad-norm spikes cause loss jumps. Raise modestly if clipping fires almost every step and is throttling progress.
gradient_accumulation_steps Accumulate grads over micro-batches to reach a large effective batch under memory limits. As needed (1–many) Increase to grow effective batch without more memory; decrease for fresher, more frequent updates.
optimizer (AdamW) betas Momentum (β1) and variance (β2) decay. β2 lower than default stabilizes noisy RL gradients. β1 0.9; β2 0.95–0.999 Lower β2 (e.g. 0.95) if gradients are noisy/heavy-tailed and Adam is overshooting.
Adam eps Numerical floor in the Adam denominator. 1e-8 (1e-5 for low-precision) Raise if you see instability in low-precision (bf16) training.
weight_decay L2-style regularization on weights. 0.0–0.1 Add mild decay if overfitting the reward; keep 0 if it harms learning.
precision / dtype fp32 / bf16 / fp16 compute. Affects stability and throughput. bf16 for LLMs (fp32 master where needed) Move log-prob/KL math to higher precision if you see NaNs or unstable ratios in fp16.

7. Reward configuration¶

Parameter Effect Normal range When to adjust
reward shaping weights Relative weight of each reward component (e.g. correctness vs. format vs. length). Correctness dominant; auxiliaries small Rebalance if an easy auxiliary term creates a local optimum (model farms the easy bonus, ignores the hard goal).
dense vs. sparse reward Graded partial credit vs. all-or-nothing. Dense rewards give gradient before the policy can solve the task. Dense/shaped early; can sparsen later Densify when correctness never improves while format saturates (the sparse-reward trap).
length penalty / normalization Penalize or normalize by completion length to kill length-bias hacking. Mild penalty or token-level normalization Add when mean response length / truncation rate drifts upward.
reward clipping / normalization Bound or standardize reward values for stable advantages. Clip to a band; normalize to O(1) Apply when reward magnitudes are heavy-tailed and destabilize gradients.
reward-model refresh / ensembling (RLHF) Update or ensemble a learned reward model to resist over-optimization. Periodic refresh / small ensemble Use when the train–eval gap widens (the policy is exploiting RM quirks).

8. Variant-specific knobs (DAPO / Dr. GRPO / dynamic sampling)¶

Parameter Effect Normal range When to adjust
dynamic sampling Discard prompts whose group is all-correct or all-wrong (zero advantage) and resample, so every batch carries gradient. On for efficiency Enable when a large fraction of groups are zero-variance and wasting compute.
overlong filtering / soft punishment Mask or softly penalize completions that hit the length cap, instead of rewarding them. On for long-CoT tasks Enable when truncated completions corrupt the reward signal.
token-level policy loss Average the loss over tokens rather than sequences so long completions aren't down-weighted. On for long, variable-length outputs Switch when long-answer tasks show length/format bias under per-sequence averaging.
clip-higher (decoupled upper clip) See §3 — DAPO's asymmetric clip to preserve exploration. high bound ≈ 0.22–0.28 Enable alongside entropy measures when collapse is the failure mode.
no-KL / verifiable-reward mode Drop the reference KL entirely for fully verifiable rewards (math/code), leaning on clipping + dynamic sampling. Off unless reward is verifiable Use only with trustworthy programmatic rewards; monitor drift in general ability.

Quick starting point (verifiable-reward GRPO on a pretrained LLM)¶

A reasonable default set to tune from, not a universal recipe:

  • group_size 8–16, temperature 1.0, top_p 1.0
  • norm_std off (Dr. GRPO), token-level loss
  • clip_eps 0.2 (consider clip_high 0.28 if entropy collapses)
  • inner_epochs 1
  • kl_beta 0.0–0.04 with k3 estimator; reference fixed at the SFT checkpoint
  • ent_coef 0–0.01 (anneal if used)
  • lr ~1e-6 with short warmup, max_grad_norm 1.0, bf16
  • dynamic sampling on; dense reward early, length-normalized

Change one thing at a time, and let the metrics sheet tell you which knob the current failure mode points to.
